The American Endeavor

The Architecture of a New American Century

I believe in the indomitable American Spirit.

I believe that the United States is not just a place on a map, but a project—a constant, messy, magnificent struggle to build a more perfect union. I believe that at our best, this nation has been the single greatest force for human dignity in history. We are the country that broke the back of Fascism, rebuilt Europe from the ashes, and lit the torch of liberty that inspired the world.

But I believe that since 2016, we have faltered.

We have allowed the politics of fear to shrink our horizon. We have retreated from the world stage, leaving vacuums filled by tyrants. We have turned on each other at home, trading the “E Pluribus Unum” of our founding for a fractured tribalism. We have forgotten that America does not hide behind walls; America tears them down.

I believe it is time to lead again.

This is not a time for cynicism. This is a time for a Universalist Realism—a doctrine that is clear-eyed about the threats we face, but unshakeable in the values we defend. It means applying a single standard of human rights to all nations, including our own. It means projecting American power not to dominate, but to stabilize. It means competing fiercely with our adversaries while keeping the door to diplomacy open. It means we can arm Ukraine and criticize Israeli settlements in the same breath, because principle does not pick favorites. It is the rejection of both naive idealism and cynical realpolitik in favor of principled, strategic engagement.

I believe that a nation cannot credibly champion freedom abroad while tolerating injustice at home. I believe that domestic strength—a middle class that thrives, a justice system that protects, an economy that includes—is the foundation upon which our global leadership rests. And I believe that when we get both right, when we project power with principle and govern at home with equity, we are not just a country. We are the proof that self-governance works.

Democracy, Civil Rights & The Social Contract

My Statement on Civil Rights & State Oppression

I believe in a “New Civil Rights Act” for the 21st Century.

I unequivocally support the 14th Amendment’s guarantee of equal protection under the law. I advocate for a new, federally codified Civil Rights Act that explicitly enshrines marriage equality, protects LGBTQ+ individuals from discrimination, and outlaws systemic inequities like redlining. I believe these rights are federal mandates, not state suggestions. I support federal preemption to make discriminatory state crackdowns illegal, ensuring that a citizen’s fundamental rights do not vanish when they cross a state line.

I demand the codification of immigrant rights.

I reject the legal gray zones that allow for the abuse of non-citizens. I believe that all persons within the United States—regardless of status—are entitled to the full protections of the Constitution. This includes the 1st (Speech), 4th (Search & Seizure), 5th/6th (Due Process), and 8th (Cruel & Unusual Punishment) Amendments. Due process is not a privilege of citizenship; it is a check on government power that must apply to everyone.

Medical Rights & Bodily Autonomy

My Statement on Medical Rights & Bodily Autonomy

I believe in codifying the Roe v. Wade standard into federal law.

I believe that medical decisions belong in a doctor’s office, not a state legislature. I advocate for the immediate passage of a federal statute (such as the Women’s Health Protection Act) that codifies the protections of Roe v. Wade. This would establish a national baseline where abortion is legal and protected up to the point of fetal viability. This protection must explicitly extend to medication abortion—including mifepristone—which has become the frontline of legislative and judicial attack and represents the most immediate access crisis facing millions of Americans today.

I reject the government’s interference in the exam room.

While I support the ability of states to regulate post-viability procedures consistent with the Roe framework, I fundamentally reject any government attempt to ban care before viability or to criminalize doctors for saving a woman’s life. The government has no place inserting itself between a patient and their physician.

Transgender Rights

I believe Trans Rights are Human Rights.

I reject the demonization of transgender Americans and support their right to live openly and authentically. I advocate for a science-based approach to gender-affirming care that respects the complexity of medical transition.

I distinguish between Social, Medical, and Surgical care.

  • For Minors: I wholeheartedly support access to social transition (names, pronouns, clothing) and reversible medical interventions like puberty blockers, which allow youth and families time to make informed decisions.
  • For Surgeries: I believe that irreversible surgical interventions should be reserved for adults (18+). Surgical transition for minors should not be standard practice and should only be considered in extraordinary circumstances where a multidisciplinary medical team deems it strictly necessary for the immediate safety and well-being of the patient.

I believe in “Science-Based Inclusion” for athletics.

  • In Co-Ed Sports: I support full inclusion without restriction.
  • In Gender-Segregated Sports: I support the participation of trans athletes after a clinically appropriate period of hormone replacement therapy (HRT). Research indicates that after 1 to 3 years of testosterone suppression, significant athletic advantages in hemoglobin and endurance diminish to levels comparable with cisgender athletes. I believe league policies should be based on these biometric benchmarks, not on blanket bans that target vulnerable children.

Statehood & Representation

My Statement on Statehood & Representation

I believe that taxation without representation is tyranny.

I support full statehood for Washington D.C. The denial of voting representation to over 700,000 American citizens is an abhorrent contradiction to the founding principles of our nation. I reject the partisan excuses used to disenfranchise the capital’s residents and believe they deserve equal standing in the Union.

I advocate for Puerto Rican statehood based on economic integration.

I support an immediate, binding territory-wide referendum on statehood for Puerto Rico. I believe the trigger for statehood should be tied to the transition of the territory into the federal income tax system. Given the island’s population size, its deep historical contribution to the US, and the necessity of economic investment, statehood is the only logical path to end its colonial status and unleash its economic potential.

I propose a “Territorial Compact” for our island neighbors.

For smaller territories with populations under 500,000—such as Guam, American Samoa, the USVI, and the CNMI—I advocate for a unified political compact. While retaining their individual national identities, distinct cultural sovereignty, and full local governance, I believe these territories should be empowered to elect a single, shared voting representative to the U.S. House—much as a small state holds a single at-large seat—whose mandate is to represent their collective interests in federal legislation. Each territory would additionally retain its own non-voting delegate for territory-specific affairs.

I recognize that the four territories have meaningfully different legal relationships with the federal government—including American Samoa’s unique citizenship and land tenure framework—and the compact must be structured to accommodate, rather than override, those distinctions. I acknowledge this proposal would likely require a constitutional amendment or, at minimum, a creative application of Article IV, and I accept that legal complexity as the cost of finally enfranchising nearly 350,000 Americans who currently have no voice in the laws that govern them. The purpose of this compact is not homogenization, but amplification.

Voting Rights & Electoral Integrity

My Statement on Voting Rights & Electoral Integrity

I believe the right to vote is the cornerstone of all other rights.

A democracy that makes it harder to vote is not a democracy—it is a managed oligarchy dressed in the language of the republic. I support the full restoration and expansion of the Voting Rights Act, including a modernized preclearance requirement that prevents states with documented histories of voter suppression from enacting new restrictions without federal review. Every eligible American citizen should be able to cast their ballot without navigating an obstacle course designed to exhaust them into giving up.

I advocate for a “Universal Ballot Access” standard.

I believe the federal government must establish a national baseline for ballot access that no state may fall below. This includes: automatic voter registration for all eligible citizens at the point of contact with any government agency; a minimum of fifteen early voting days in all federal elections; vote-by-mail availability for all citizens upon request; and the restoration of voting rights for formerly incarcerated individuals upon completion of their sentence. Civic participation is not a reward for perfect behavior—it is the foundation of a legitimate government.

I support a national free voter ID program.

I refuse to accept the false choice between election security and voter access. I believe that if any level of government requires identification to vote, it must simultaneously provide that identification free of charge, automatically, and with minimal bureaucratic burden. I support a federally issued, free voter identification card available to all eligible citizens—eliminating the access barrier that voter ID laws impose on low-income, elderly, and minority voters while rendering the “voter fraud” argument moot. The solution to the voter ID debate is not to fight over whether ID should be required; it is to ensure that no American is ever denied the franchise because they cannot afford to prove who they are.

I demand an end to partisan gerrymandering.

I believe the practice of politicians drawing their own district lines is a fundamental corruption of representative democracy. I support the establishment of independent, nonpartisan redistricting commissions in all fifty states for both congressional and state legislative maps. When politicians choose their voters rather than voters choosing their representatives, the social contract is broken.

I believe we must modernize and protect our election infrastructure.

I support robust federal funding for the modernization of voting systems, the mandatory use of paper ballot backups in all federal elections, and the independence of the Election Assistance Commission from partisan interference. I believe election administration should be treated as critical national security infrastructure—because it is.

Justice & Rehabilitation

My Statement on Justice & Rehabilitation

I condemn the failure of mass incarceration.

I believe that a justice system that releases people worse off than when they entered is a broken system. I advocate for the repeal of mandatory minimums and the 1994 Crime Bill provisions that stripped judges of discretion. I support a “Justice Reinvestment” strategy that ties federal prison funding to recidivism rates—prisons should be paid for rehabilitating citizens, not warehousing bodies.

I support the full legalization of cannabis.

I believe the “War on Drugs” has been a catastrophic failure that has disproportionately devastated BIPOC communities. I advocate for the full federal descheduling and legalization of cannabis. I believe the tax revenue generated from this new industry should be earmarked for the “New Civil Rights Act” enforcement and for grants to minority-owned small businesses, repairing the economic damage of decades of prohibition.

I believe the Federal Death Penalty must be reserved for the “Worst of the Worst.”

I oppose the broad application of capital punishment, but I support its retention for the most egregious offenses against the state and humanity. I hold this position as a deliberate moral compromise, not a philosophical certainty. I recognize the profound difficulty of declaring any human being beyond redemption with the certainty that taking their life demands—and I am acutely aware of the documented risk of executing the innocent. My support for a narrowly tailored death penalty is therefore conditional: it must never be applied to the mentally ill, to those with unresolved questions of innocence, or to any case short of the clearest, most overwhelming evidence of guilt. I believe the taking of a life is the most immense responsibility a government can bear, and I refuse to allow prosecutorial habit or political pressure to cheapen that gravity.

I advocate for “Narrow Tailoring” of the ultimate penalty.

I believe the death penalty should be strictly limited to cases of:

  • High Treason: Acts that betray the nation and jeopardize its survival.
  • Terrorism: Mass casualty attacks that target innocent civilians.
  • Serial Killers: Offenders who have demonstrated a pathological compulsion to kill with no hope of redemption.

For all other crimes, I believe in life imprisonment without parole. We must ensure that the state does not execute the innocent, the mentally ill, or the redeemable.

Police Reform & Community Safety

My Statement on Police Reform & Community Safety

I believe in both the necessity of law enforcement and the obligation to reform it.

I reject the false binary of “back the blue” versus “defund the police.” This is a manufactured choice designed to prevent serious conversation. I believe that effective, accountable, and community-rooted policing is a civil right—particularly for the communities that suffer most from both crime and police misconduct. The goal is not fewer police; it is better policing, tethered to the communities it serves and accountable to the law it enforces.

I demand an end to qualified immunity as a blanket shield.

The doctrine of qualified immunity—which requires plaintiffs to identify a virtually identical prior case to hold an officer liable for unconstitutional conduct—has become a near-total barrier to civil accountability for police misconduct. I support federal legislation reforming qualified immunity to restore meaningful civil recourse for victims of unconstitutional policing, while preserving reasonable good-faith protections for officers acting within clearly established law. Accountability is not anti-police; it is the foundation of public trust.

I support a federal use-of-force standard.

The United States has no national standard governing when and how law enforcement may use lethal or non-lethal force. I advocate for a federal minimum standard requiring that force be used only as a last resort, that de-escalation techniques be mandatory training requirements, and that chokeholds and other high-risk restraint techniques be banned in all non-lethal encounters. This standard would apply to all agencies receiving federal law enforcement funding.

I demand mandatory body-worn cameras with enforceable activation policies.

Body-worn cameras are among the most broadly supported police accountability tools in America—supported by the public, by civil rights organizations, and by the majority of rank-and-file officers who know that footage protects the honest as much as it exposes the corrupt. I support federal funding for universal body camera deployment in all departments receiving federal law enforcement grants, paired with strict policies mandating activation during all enforcement encounters. Deliberate deactivation or obstruction of camera footage during an encounter must carry meaningful disciplinary and evidentiary consequences. If an officer’s body camera is off during a use-of-force incident, the evidentiary presumption should not favor the officer.

I believe in reinvesting in the full ecosystem of public safety.

Policing alone cannot solve the conditions that breed crime. I support significant federal investment in mental health crisis response—including co-responder programs that pair trained clinicians with officers on mental health calls—as well as violence interruption programs, community-based conflict resolution, and the social services infrastructure (housing, addiction treatment, youth programming) that prevents the conditions that generate crime in the first place. We do not make communities safer by cutting the safety net and then arresting the people who fall through it.

I support the creation of a National Police Misconduct Registry.

Officers with documented histories of excessive force, dishonesty, or civil rights violations currently face no systemic barrier to being hired by a new department across a state line. I advocate for a federally maintained, publicly accessible registry of sustained officer misconduct findings—not allegations, but findings—to ensure that bad actors cannot simply relocate their badges.

Federal Agent Accountability & The Duty to Disobey

My Statement on Federal Agent Accountability & The Duty to Disobey

I believe there must be a reckoning for what was done in our name.

During the second Trump administration, elements within Immigration and Customs Enforcement and the Department of Homeland Security conducted enforcement operations that violated the fundamental human and civil rights of immigrants, peaceful protesters, and American citizens. People were detained without due process. Families were destroyed. Nonviolent individuals who posed no threat to anyone died in federal custody or at the hands of federal agents. These were not the actions of a few rogue officers—they were the product of an institutional culture that prioritized cruelty as deterrence and treated human beings as political props. I refuse to allow this chapter to close without consequence.

I support the passage of a “Restoring Justice and Federal Accountability Act.”

I advocate for a comprehensive legislative framework that moves beyond the hollow rituals of administrative leave and reassignment and instead imposes real, personal accountability on every agent, supervisor, and official who participated in, authorized, or enabled enforcement actions that resulted in the death, wrongful detention, or injury of nonviolent individuals, peaceful demonstrators, or American citizens who posed no threat.

I demand the removal of qualified immunity for agents who violated constitutional rights.

I support a statutory cause of action that allows survivors and victims’ families to personally sue federal agents who participated in these abuses. The shield of qualified immunity—which currently requires victims to identify a nearly identical prior case before they can hold an officer accountable—must be stripped from those who committed these acts. I reject the legal fiction that an agent who brutalizes an innocent person should be protected because no court has previously ruled on that exact method of brutalization. The Constitution is not a technicality.

I believe those who profited from cruelty should fund the restoration of their victims.

I advocate for the mandatory forfeiture of all federal retirement annuities, pensions, Thrift Savings Plan holdings, and accumulated government benefits from agents found to have participated in these violations. These forfeited assets must not revert to the general Treasury—they must be placed in a dedicated Victims’ Reparation Escrow and used exclusively to provide direct financial reparations to survivors, to the families of those who were killed, and to individuals who were wrongfully detained, deported, or injured. Justice is not abstract; it is material. The people who were harmed deserve to be made whole, and the people who harmed them should bear the cost.

I demand that the federal government refuse to shield those who broke their oath.

Under current practice, when a federal agent is sued for misconduct, the Department of Justice provides their legal defense and the taxpayer pays any resulting judgment. I reject this arrangement for agents covered under this Act. The government must be statutorily prohibited from providing legal counsel, indemnification, or payment of judgments for agents found to have violated civil and human rights. These debts must be satisfied from the personal assets, savings, and future earnings of the individuals responsible—not from the pockets of the taxpayers they victimized. Furthermore, I support amending the Bankruptcy Code to make these judgments permanently nondischargeable. You do not get to destroy a family and then walk away clean through a bankruptcy filing.

I support the termination and permanent debarment of offending agents.

Any federal employee found to have participated in the specified enforcement actions must be removed from service—not suspended, not reassigned, not permitted to quietly resign with benefits. Removed. I further support a lifetime bar on federal employment and a prohibition on federal contractors hiring these individuals. Companies that employ debarred agents should face the immediate termination of their government contracts. Public trust, once this fundamentally violated, cannot be restored by a transfer to a different desk.

I reject—absolutely and without exception—the defense of “following orders.”

The principle that obedience to an unlawful order does not absolve the individual of responsibility is not new. It was established at Nuremberg. It is embedded in the Uniform Code of Military Justice. And yet it has never been formally codified in federal civilian employment law. I support its codification now. Every federal agent swears an oath to support and defend the Constitution—not to support and defend their supervisor’s directives. When an order is manifestly unconstitutional, the duty of a sworn officer is to refuse it. Those who followed unlawful orders will be held to the same standard as those who gave them. Moral courage is not optional in public service.

I believe in due process—even for those who denied it to others.

I am not proposing a purge. I support the establishment of an independent Federal Agent Accountability Review Board—composed of agency representatives and presidentially appointed civil rights experts—to hear the appeals of agents facing termination and forfeiture. Every individual must have the opportunity to present evidence that they did not participate in the actions at issue. The standard of review must be fair: the agency bears the burden of proving participation by a preponderance of the evidence. But I insist on one structural safeguard: if participation is proven, the Board must not have the authority to mitigate the penalty. This is not a negotiation. The consequences are fixed. The only question is whether the facts apply.

I believe this framework must outlast any single administration.

I am not interested in political revenge. I am interested in establishing a permanent legal precedent: that federal agents who violate the constitutional rights of the people they are sworn to serve will face personal financial ruin, criminal referral, and permanent exclusion from public service. This is not about one president or one agency. It is about ensuring that no future administration can weaponize federal law enforcement against its own people and expect the agents who carry out that weaponization to be protected by the very government they disgraced. The message must be unmistakable: the Constitution is not suspended by executive order, and the oath you swore is not a suggestion.

Education & Human Capital

My Statement on Education & Human Capital

I believe in a “Human Capital Strategy” to compete globally.

I advocate for the full funding of public education, viewing it as a national security imperative. We cannot out-compete nations like China if we do not out-educate them. I support empowering teachers with the autonomy and materials they need, and I demand they be compensated as the nation-builders they are. A teacher’s salary should reflect their immense contribution to our future economy and stability. Specifically, I support establishing a federal minimum salary floor for public school teachers—indexed to regional cost of living—and a federal supplemental pay program for teachers in high-need and rural districts, ensuring that geography does not determine whether a child has an experienced educator in front of them.

I believe in a dual-track focus on STEM and Vocational Trades.

I reject the idea that a four-year university degree is the only path to success. I support massive federal investment in STEM education to keep us on the cutting edge of science, alongside robust funding for Vocational and Technical programs. We need engineers to design the future, but we also need the skilled tradespeople to build it. My industrial policy requires a workforce ready to weld, code, and construct.

I support “The Associate’s Guarantee.”

I believe that the first two years of higher education—whether at a Community College or a State University—should be tuition-free. This ensures that every American can obtain an Associate’s Degree or a Trade Certification without crushing debt. This is the most efficient way to close the skills gap and expand the middle class.

I believe public education is the forge of democratic citizenship.

I reject the coordinated campaign to ban books, sanitize history, and transform public school curricula into instruments of political conformity. A child who is not taught the full, honest history of their country—including its failures, its injustices, and the movements that fought to correct them—is not being educated; they are being indoctrinated into comfortable ignorance. I support the academic freedom of teachers and librarians to select age-appropriate materials that reflect the full breadth of the American experience, and I oppose any legislation that prohibits the discussion of race, gender, or historical injustice in public schools under the guise of “parental rights.”

I distinguish between parental involvement and political censorship.

I believe parents have every right to be involved in their children’s education—to attend school board meetings, to review curricula, to supplement classroom learning at home. What I reject is the weaponization of “parental rights” as a legislative vehicle for removing books from libraries, banning the acknowledgment of LGBTQ+ families, or preventing teachers from discussing the history of slavery, segregation, and the civil rights movement in honest terms. Education is not indoctrination simply because it makes adults uncomfortable. A democracy that fears its own history will not survive long enough to improve upon it.

Diversity, Inclusion & The American Superpower

My Statement on Diversity, Inclusion, and the American Superpower

I believe in the absolute moral imperative of human dignity.

I believe in the inherent worth of every human being, regardless of their background, the color of their skin, or whom they love. The fundamental premise of the American project is that all people are created equal. BIPOC and LGBTQ+ Americans contribute immeasurably to every aspect of our nation, and fully including them is not just a strategic benefit—it is a moral imperative.

I reject the right-wing demonization of diversity.

I wholeheartedly believe that diversity, accessibility, and inclusion are American strengths, not detriments. For too long, the political right has demonized diversity as an attack on white Americans. I understand the anxieties that drive this backlash: for some, inclusion feels like a threat to entrenched positions of privilege and power, while others have been fed the fabricated fear that DEI policies inherently lower standards or compromise safety and performance. I must be unequivocal: these positions are factually incorrect and morally abhorrent. Inclusion does not dilute excellence; it expands the pool from which excellence can be drawn.

History proves diversity is our structural superpower.

Diversity is the bedrock of our culture, our military, and our economy. Some of the greatest technological and societal leaps in human history—both within the United States and across the globe—have been engineered by minds from every conceivable background. Innovation is not confined by race, gender, sexual orientation, or origin. Our modern world has been fundamentally shaped by the brilliance of women, LGBTQ+ pioneers, immigrants, and individuals from every walk of life whose unique lived experiences allowed them to solve complex problems others couldn’t even see. Whether we are talking about the foundational algorithms of computing, breakthroughs in medical science, or the architectural frameworks of human rights, progress relies on this exact collision of different perspectives. Furthermore, people of all colors and backgrounds have bravely served in every branch of our military since the very founding of the Republic. To fully include every American in the fabric of our country is not a concession; it is the ultimate optimization of our national potential.

Humanity is a beautiful tapestry of cultures, experience, and history; it is the purposeful weaving of these unique threads that adds indispensable strength to our social fabric, our economy, and the core spirit of our Nation.

I believe disability rights are civil rights—full stop.

The Americans with Disabilities Act was landmark legislation, but the work it began is far from finished. Over thirty-five years after its passage, Americans with disabilities still face employment rates roughly half that of their non-disabled peers, persistent barriers to physical and digital accessibility, and the unconscionable continuation of the subminimum wage under Section 14(c) of the Fair Labor Standards Act—a provision that allows employers to pay disabled workers pennies on the dollar. I support the immediate repeal of Section 14(c) and the establishment of a transition framework that moves sheltered workshops toward competitive integrated employment. I advocate for the rigorous enforcement of ADA accessibility standards in both physical spaces and digital platforms—because a “Digital Bill of Rights” that does not account for the 61 million Americans living with disabilities is not universal. I believe that a society’s commitment to human dignity is measured not by how it treats its most powerful members, but by how it includes its most marginalized.

I believe in true equal protection under the law.

I firmly believe in the core principles of DEI and affirmative action as necessary tools to dismantle historic, systemic barriers and ensure accessibility. However, as a believer in equal protection, I also acknowledge that the execution of these policies must remain strictly focused on expanding opportunity. When I speak of overcorrection, I am referring to a narrow and specific concern: admission or hiring systems that impose hard numerical quotas by race—rather than holistic, opportunity-expanding review processes—have, in isolated documented cases, created structural disadvantages on the basis of race that mirror the very discrimination we seek to eradicate. This is not an argument against DEI; it is an argument for designing DEI programs that can withstand legal and moral scrutiny because they are grounded in access and opportunity, not in reductive arithmetic. True equity means leveling the playing field for everyone, ensuring that American meritocracy is truly accessible to all, without prejudice or retaliation.

Gun Safety & The Second Amendment

My Statement on Gun Safety & The Second Amendment

I fundamentally believe in the Second Amendment.

I support the individual right of law-abiding citizens to own firearms for defense and sport. I reject the notion that we must choose between the Second Amendment and public safety; I believe we can and must have both.

I advocate for “Rights with Responsibilities.”

I believe in the strict enforcement of the National Firearms Act (NFA) and I support a federal framework that includes:

  • Universal Background Checks to ensure guns don’t fall into the wrong hands.
  • Mandatory Waiting Periods to prevent impulsive violence and suicides.
  • Federal Red Flag Laws that allow for the temporary, due-process removal of firearms from individuals deemed a danger to themselves or others.

I recognize that this framework does not address assault-style weapons and high-capacity magazines—a subject of profound urgency for the communities that have buried children, parishioners, and concertgoers in the wake of mass shootings. I do not dismiss the devastation wrought by weapons designed for battlefields being used in American classrooms and churches, and I hold no philosophical attachment to their civilian availability. However, I believe the political capital required to pass and sustain an assault weapons ban—given current Supreme Court jurisprudence and the near-certain legal challenges it would face—would be more effectively invested in the universal background check, waiting period, and red flag infrastructure that have a realistic path to passage and a proven record of saving lives. This is not a permanent concession; it is a strategic sequencing. I am committed to revisiting the question of weapons of war in civilian hands once the foundational infrastructure of gun safety is built and defended.

What makes this war not merely reckless but unconscionable is its timing. On February 27—the day before the strikes began—Oman’s Foreign Minister publicly announced that a diplomatic breakthrough had been reached. Iran had reportedly agreed to never stockpile enriched uranium and to submit to full IAEA verification—the core demand of every nonproliferation framework the United States has pursued for two decades. Peace was, by the mediator’s own account, within reach. The United States and Israel chose to launch a war instead. This was not preemption born of necessity; it was a choice to destroy a diplomatic solution in favor of a military one, made by leaders who had already demonstrated their contempt for negotiated outcomes.

The consequences have been immediate and devastating. Iran has retaliated with missile and drone strikes across the Gulf region, hitting targets in Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, the UAE, and Israel. The Strait of Hormuz—through which approximately one-fifth of the world’s crude oil flows—has been effectively closed by Iranian mining operations and naval confrontation, triggering a global energy crisis that is driving inflation, forcing developing nations to ration power, and punishing the very working families that this manifesto pledges to protect. The human cost is staggering. The economic cost is global. And the strategic cost—the destruction of American credibility as a negotiating partner—may take a generation to repair.

I believe the United States has an obligation to account for the destruction it has caused.

My doctrine of Universalist Realism does not permit me to demand that Israel pay restitution for the destruction of Palestinian infrastructure while remaining silent about American bombs falling on Iranian hospitals, water treatment plants, and UNESCO World Heritage Sites. The same standard applies. If the United States has destroyed civilian infrastructure in Iran—and the evidence from the World Health Organization, the International Committee of the Red Cross, and independent journalism overwhelmingly indicates that it has—then the United States has a legal and moral obligation to participate in the reconstruction of what it destroyed.

I support the establishment of a congressionally authorized Iranian Civilian Reconstruction Fund, financed through a dedicated allocation within the defense budget, to provide humanitarian assistance and infrastructure reconstruction to Iranian civilians harmed by American military operations. This is not charity; it is the cost of the war. A nation that can afford to build the bombs can afford to rebuild what those bombs destroyed. To do otherwise is to confirm every accusation that American power is wielded without accountability—and to guarantee that the next generation of Iranians has every reason to see us as their enemy.

I demand a return to diplomacy, economic integration, and a durable peace framework.

The United States must pivot decisively away from military adventurism and commit to the hard, unglamorous, generational work of rebuilding diplomatic trust with Iran. This will not be easy. This war has shattered the credibility of American commitments and empowered the hardliners in Tehran who always argued that negotiation with Washington was futile. But the alternative—permanent war or permanent hostility with a nation of 90 million people sitting atop the world’s most critical energy chokepoint—is not a strategy; it is a slow-motion catastrophe.

I advocate for the following specific diplomatic and economic framework:

  • Immediate Ceasefire and Humanitarian Corridor: The United States must pursue an immediate, unconditional ceasefire and the establishment of humanitarian corridors for medical supplies, food, and clean water to Iranian civilians affected by the bombing campaign. This is a prerequisite for any subsequent diplomatic engagement.
  • Resumption of Mediated Negotiations: I support the immediate resumption of negotiations through the Omani mediation channel that was producing results before it was destroyed by the February 28 strikes. Oman has served as a trusted intermediary for decades, and the diplomatic infrastructure it built must be restored, not abandoned.
  • A JCPOA Successor Agreement: I support the negotiation of a comprehensive successor to the JCPOA—one that addresses nuclear enrichment, ballistic missile development, and regional proxy activity, while providing Iran with verifiable sanctions relief, economic integration pathways, and the security guarantees that the original agreement lacked. The failure of the JCPOA was not in its design but in the American decision to abandon it; the successor must include multilateral enforcement mechanisms that make it resistant to unilateral withdrawal by any single party.
  • Economic Integration as Strategic Architecture: I reject the premise that sanctions alone produce democratic reform; decades of evidence suggest the opposite—sanctions entrench autocratic regimes by giving them an external enemy to blame while punishing the civilian population. I support a structured economic normalization framework—modeled on the approach that transformed post-Soviet Eastern Europe—that ties incremental sanctions relief to specific, verifiable benchmarks: the release of political prisoners, the restoration of press freedoms, the cessation of support for designated terrorist organizations, and the acceptance of IAEA inspection protocols. The goal is not to reward the regime but to empower the Iranian people, who have repeatedly demonstrated—in the Green Movement of 2009, the Woman, Life, Freedom protests of 2022, and the January 2026 uprising—that they want a democratic future.
  • Strait of Hormuz International Freedom of Navigation Compact: The effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz is an economic crisis that extends far beyond the Middle East. I support the negotiation of a multilateral freedom of navigation agreement—including the Gulf states, Iran, and the major trading nations whose economies depend on the strait—that guarantees unimpeded commercial transit, establishes a joint maritime monitoring mechanism, and removes the strait from the chessboard of bilateral military confrontation. The global economy cannot be held hostage to a single chokepoint, and the solution is diplomatic architecture, not perpetual naval standoff.

I recognize the complexity of Iran’s post-Khamenei political landscape.

The assassination of Ali Khamenei has not produced the regime collapse that the architects of this war evidently expected. His son, Mojtaba Khamenei, has been installed as Supreme Leader, and hundreds of thousands of Iranians have rallied in support of the new leadership—not necessarily because they endorse theocratic rule, but because they have been given a war that unifies them against an external enemy. This is the oldest and most predictable consequence of military aggression against a sovereign nation: you do not liberate a people by bombing them. You radicalize them.

American diplomacy must engage with the Iranian government as it exists, not as we wish it to be. This does not mean endorsing the Islamic Republic’s domestic repression or its regional proxy network. It means accepting the fundamental diplomatic reality that you negotiate peace with your adversaries, not with your friends, and that the precondition for engagement cannot be the prior capitulation of the other side. The Iranian people’s struggle for democratic self-determination is their own; American foreign policy should support it through economic opportunity and diplomatic engagement, not through the barrel of a gun that has only succeeded in rallying the population behind the very regime we claim to oppose.

Russia, Ukraine & Global Sovereignty

I condemn the Russian Federation as a rogue actor on the world stage.

I view the Russian state under its current totalitarian leadership as a fundamental threat to global order and international law. I reject its history of invasions, election interference, funding of breakaway movements, and illegal annexations. I believe that Russia has proven it does not respect the sovereignty of its neighbors and that its actions are driven not by legitimate security concerns, but by the ego and imperialist will of a dictator.

I support comprehensive military and economic assistance to Ukraine.

I do not believe in half-measures when facing a war of aggression. I support providing Ukraine—and other nations threatened by Russia—with both defensive and offensive military capabilities. We cannot allow a sovereign democracy to be erased simply to appease an aggressor. I advocate for full intelligence sharing, robust economic aid, and the delivery of the necessary firepower to expel invading forces and restore recognized borders.

I steadfastly support the NATO Open Door policy.

I believe that every nation has the sovereign right to choose its own alliances. No country should be able to veto the security decisions of its neighbors. I support the expansion of NATO as a defensive bulwark against authoritarianism and believe the door must remain open to Ukraine and other European democracies seeking protection from Russian aggression.

I believe that unchecked aggression invites global chaos.

To allow Russia to violently redraw maps and subjugate free peoples is to accept a world where “might makes right.” I refuse to accept that world. Supporting the military defeat of Russian aggression is not “warmongering”; it is the necessary defense of the principles of liberty, self-determination, and the rule of law.

The Western Hemisphere

I believe in engagement over isolation.

I advocate for the full normalization of relations with Cuba, including the re-establishment of economic and political ties. The policy of isolation has failed to produce democracy and has instead turned a neighbor into a strategic outpost for our adversaries. By engaging with the Cuban people and opening their society to Western economies, we can displace the influence of Russia, China, and Iran in our own hemisphere. Peace and trade are more effective tools for change than embargoes.

I reject the “Spheres of Influence” doctrine.

I fundamentally disagree with the revival of the Monroe Doctrine and the idea that the Western Hemisphere is the backyard of the United States. I reject the “Great Power” model of geopolitics which suggests that superpowers have the right to dominate their respective regions. Just as Russia has no right to dominate Ukraine, the United States has no right to dictate the sovereignty of Latin American nations.

I oppose the history of US interventionism.

I condemn the legacy of US interference in Central and South America, whether through CIA operations, regime change, or destabilizing NGOs. History has shown that when the US engineers instability in its neighbors, the result is often the rise of totalitarian regimes, civil strife, and long-term regional chaos.

I believe in partnership, not domination.

The stability of the Western Hemisphere depends on mutual respect and economic cooperation, not coercion. We secure our neighborhood by being a reliable partner that supports the self-determination of other nations, rather than an overlord that fears their independence.

Venezuela and Central America demand a regional response.

The collapse of Venezuela under authoritarian misrule has produced one of the largest displacement crises in the Western Hemisphere’s modern history—over seven million refugees and migrants have fled, straining the resources and social fabric of Colombia, Peru, Brazil, and every nation in their path. I support robust US humanitarian assistance to host countries, diplomatic pressure for free and fair elections in Venezuela, and a structured legal pathway for Venezuelan refugees already in the United States. Punishing refugees for the sins of their government is not policy—it is cruelty.

Cartel violence is a shared crisis.

The drug cartels that terrorize communities south of our border are fueled by American demand for narcotics and armed by American firearms trafficking southward. This is not someone else’s problem—it is a co-produced catastrophe. I support a comprehensive strategy that includes robust interdiction cooperation with Mexican and Central American governments, a dramatic increase in efforts to stop the illegal flow of US weapons south, investment in the economic development and rule of law institutions in the Northern Triangle countries, and the treatment of addiction within our own borders as the demand-reduction strategy it is. We cannot secure our southern border by ignoring the conditions that drive people to cross it.

The Asia-Pacific & The Rise of China

I believe in an “Eyes Wide Open” relationship with China.

I recognize the People’s Republic of China as an authoritarian state with expansionist ambitions that challenge democratic values. However, I reject the idea of a new Cold War that cuts off all communication. Unlike the reckless aggression of Russia, China is deeply integrated into the global community. I advocate for open, honest, and direct diplomatic relations to manage competition and ensure global stability. We must cooperate where possible (climate, trade standards) while fiercely competing where necessary (human rights, technology).

I unequivocally support the independence of Taiwan.

I reject the “One China” principle as a relic of the past that ignores the reality of modern Taiwan. The 23 million people of Taiwan have built a vibrant, sovereign democracy, and they have the absolute right to self-determination. Their future must be decided by their own ballots, not by Beijing’s threats. I support robust US support for Taiwan’s defense and its inclusion in the international community.

I reject the legitimacy of the North Korean regime.

I view the “Democratic People’s Republic of Korea” not as a legitimate government, but as a totalitarian criminal enterprise that holds its own people hostage. It is a rogue state whose nuclear aggression threatens the entire Pacific Rim. I oppose any concessions that legitimize its human rights abuses or its nuclear blackmail.

I believe our alliances are the bedrock of Pacific peace.

  • On Japan: I view Japan as our most indispensable ally in the region. Its strategic geography is the key to checking authoritarian expansion from Russia and China. The “Japanese Miracle” is proof that US partnership, when combined with democratic reconstruction, creates lasting peace and prosperity.
  • On South Korea & The Philippines: I advocate for strengthening our treaty alliances with Seoul and Manila. These partnerships are not just about military bases; they are a united front of democracies standing against coercion in the South China Sea and the Korean Peninsula.
  • On India: I view India as a critical democratic partner in the Indo-Pacific and a natural counterweight to Chinese regional dominance. The Quad partnership—linking the United States, India, Japan, and Australia—represents one of the most promising multilateral frameworks for ensuring a free and open Pacific. I support deepening US-India cooperation on defense, technology, and supply chain diversification, particularly in semiconductor manufacturing and clean energy. However, I will not pretend that strategic partnership requires silence on democratic backsliding. I am concerned by the erosion of press freedom, the treatment of religious minorities, and the centralization of executive power under the current Indian government. A true partnership between democracies must include the honest expectation that both partners live up to democratic values. India’s greatest strategic asset is not its military—it is its democratic legitimacy. I urge its leaders not to squander it.

Therefore, I am a Pacific Realist.

I believe we can talk to our adversaries without surrendering our values. I believe we can trade with China while arming Taiwan. I believe that a strong American presence in Asia is not “imperialism,” but a necessary shield for free nations living in the shadow of giants.

Global Economic Competition

I believe in “Out-Building” Autocracy.

I advocate for the United States to implement a direct, well-funded competitor to China’s Belt and Road Initiative. We cannot simply warn developing nations against Chinese debt traps; we must offer a tangible, superior alternative. I support a strategy of massive investment in the infrastructure, energy, and technology sectors of the Global South.

I believe in Economic Statecraft as a tool of partnership.

I reject the idea of abandoning the developing world to the influence of adversaries. By investing in the roads, ports, and grids of developing nations, we do not just push out Chinese influence; we ingratiate ourselves as a reliable partner. These investments should create strong, reciprocal economic ties that benefit US businesses while respecting the sovereignty and labor standards of the host nations.

I believe that presence is influence.

If the United States wants to champion democracy and stability, we must be present in the economies of the world. We secure our future not just by maintaining a superior military, but by being the world’s preferred partner in innovation and development.

The institutional vehicle for this strategy exists: the U.S. International Development Finance Corporation (DFC), established in 2018, has the mandate but has chronically lacked the funding and political will to compete at scale with Chinese Belt and Road investment. I advocate for a ten-fold increase in DFC lending capacity, the removal of bureaucratic constraints that slow deal execution, and a mandate to prioritize clean energy, digital infrastructure, and port development in Sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia, and Latin America—the regions where Chinese influence is most aggressively expanding. American dollars should build the roads that connect farmers to markets, the grids that power hospitals, and the cables that connect entrepreneurs to the global economy. That is how a democracy wins a competition of ideas.

A Closing Word

I did not write this document because I believe I have all the answers. I wrote it because I believe the American people deserve leaders who are willing to show their work—to say plainly what they believe, why they believe it, and what they intend to do about it.

Too much of our politics has become performance: slogans without substance, outrage without alternatives, promises designed to win news cycles rather than build futures.

This manifesto is my answer to that failure. It is imperfect. It will evolve. Some of these positions will be tested by events I cannot foresee, and I will adapt them—but I will not abandon the principles that underpin them.

I believe in the equal dignity of every human being. I believe in the obligation of the strong to protect the vulnerable. I believe that American power, properly directed, remains the greatest force for human freedom the world has ever known. And I believe that the measure of a nation is not the wealth of its wealthiest, but the dignity afforded to its most forgotten.

The American story is not finished.

It is not a eulogy to be delivered or a museum piece to be preserved behind glass. It is a living argument—loud, contentious, unresolved, and magnificent—about what a free people can build together when they refuse to give up on each other.

I refuse to give up.
