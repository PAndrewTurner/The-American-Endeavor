The American Economy & Human Capital

I believe in “Modern Supply-Side” economics—the government must back strategic industries, invest in its people, and build an economy that works for everyone.

Housing & Infrastructure

Ending exclusionary zoning, first-time homebuyer assistance, anti-speculation laws, high-speed rail, and energy strategy.

Healthcare

A public option, full Medicaid expansion, mental health parity, opioid crisis response, and saving rural hospitals.

Labor & Unions

The PRO Act, raising the minimum wage, gig worker protections, and restoring the power of organized labor.

Childcare & Family Policy

Universal pre-K, capping childcare costs, 12 weeks of paid family leave, and investing in care workers.

Higher Education & Debt

Full student loan jubilee, investigating the Tuition-Industrial Complex, and ending interest capitalization.

Consumer Protection

Usury caps on credit cards, eliminating junk fees, and a fully funded CFPB.

Taxation

New brackets for the ultra-wealthy, closing the unrealized gains loophole, and middle-class tax relief.

Social Security & Medicare

Lifting the payroll tax cap, aggressive drug price negotiation, and expanding Medicare to cover dental, vision, and hearing.

Trade & Tariffs

Precision Protectionism over blanket tariffs, comparative advantage, and deepening ties with allied nations.

Truth in Hiring

Banning ghost jobs, mandating wage transparency, compensating application tests, and banning AI interviews.

The American Economy: Housing & Infrastructure

My Statement on Housing & Infrastructure

I believe in an “American Blueprint” for the housing crisis.

I view the housing affordability crisis as a policy choice caused by artificial scarcity. I support conditioning federal transportation and HUD grants on the removal of exclusionary zoning laws. States and municipalities that ban “missing middle” housing (duplexes, townhomes) or enforce arbitrary parking minimums should not receive federal infrastructure dollars. I believe in building our way out of the crisis: if we want to welcome immigrants and empower the middle class, we must legalize the construction of homes.

I believe in unlocking the American Dream through federal homebuyer assistance.

I advocate for robust first-time homebuyer tax credits and federally backed downpayment assistance loans. The greatest barrier to homeownership for the working and middle class is often not the monthly mortgage, but the immense upfront capital required to close. I believe that if we want to build a true ownership society, we must bridge this wealth gap. By providing targeted federal support for new buyers, we allow young families to build generational wealth and stabilize our communities, rather than remaining trapped in a cycle of renting from corporate landlords.

I advocate for housing affordability through anti-speculation laws.

I believe homes are for families, not financial portfolios. In addition to building more supply, I support tax penalties on large institutional investors and hedge funds that buy up single-family starter homes to turn them into permanent rentals. We must level the playing field so that first-time homebuyers are not outbid by Wall Street conglomerates.

I believe infrastructure is the physical backbone of democracy.

I advocate for a “fix-it-first” policy that prioritizes the modernization of existing transit, bridges, and rail over seamless sprawl. I support massive federal investment in high-speed rail and public transit, not just to combat climate change, but to connect marginalized communities to economic opportunity. Freedom of movement is a civil right, and robust public transit is its guarantor.

I believe in rebuilding the physical arteries of American commerce.

I advocate for a massive, federally-led investment in our national infrastructure. This is not just about patching potholes; it is about modernizing the bridges, tunnels, and ports that facilitate our trade. I believe that a superpower cannot run on third-world infrastructure. From reinforcing our power grids to withstand climate disasters to expanding rural broadband and fiber access, I believe that connectivity is a prerequisite for economic opportunity in the 21st century.

I support an “All-of-the-Above” energy strategy focused on efficiency.

I believe in aggressive investment in renewable energy technologies to secure a sustainable future. I fully support the development, research, and construction of green, renewable, and clean energy, such as Solar, Wind, and Hydroelectric, while also supporting nuclear energy. However, I reject the immediate abandonment of our current energy baseline. I support investing in the modernization of non-renewable energy facilities to drastically reduce pollution and increase efficiency. We must transition intelligently, ensuring energy reliability while we build the green grid of tomorrow.

I advocate for the re-shoring of critical high-tech industries.

I view the domestic production of semiconductors and microchips not just as an economic goal, but as a national security imperative. I support federal incentives to build factories that produce high-tech components and revitalize our automotive industry. We must end our reliance on fragile foreign supply chains for the technologies that power our cars, our phones, and our defense systems.

Healthcare

My Statement on Healthcare

I believe in healthcare as a universal choice.

I support a “Public Option” that competes directly with private insurers. I believe every American should have the option to buy into a high-quality, government-run plan (like Medicare) that utilizes federal negotiating power to crush the cost of prescription drugs. I do not believe in forcing people onto a single plan, but I believe in forcing private insurance companies to compete against a non-profit alternative. If they cannot provide better care than the government at a lower cost, they do not deserve to exist.

I demand the full expansion of Medicaid in every state.

There are still states in this country that have refused to expand Medicaid under the Affordable Care Act, leaving millions of their poorest residents in a “coverage gap”—too poor to afford marketplace insurance, too “wealthy” for pre-expansion Medicaid. This is not a policy disagreement; it is a moral abdication. I support using every available federal lever—including enhanced matching rates and, if necessary, direct federal enrollment—to close this gap. No American should die of a treatable illness because their governor made a political calculation.

I believe mental health is health—and I demand parity in practice, not just in law.

The Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act has been on the books for over fifteen years, and insurers continue to violate it with impunity—imposing higher copays, stricter prior authorization requirements, and narrower provider networks for mental health than for physical health. I support aggressive federal enforcement of parity requirements, including meaningful financial penalties for insurers who systematically deny mental health claims. I also support a major federal investment in the mental health workforce pipeline—training grants, loan forgiveness, and Medicaid reimbursement rate increases—to address the catastrophic shortage of providers, particularly in rural and underserved communities.

I view the opioid and fentanyl crisis as a public health emergency, not a criminal justice problem.

The synthetic opioid epidemic has killed more Americans than any drug crisis in our history, and our response has been catastrophically inadequate. I support a public health framework that prioritizes medication-assisted treatment, harm reduction (including the expansion of naloxone access and supervised consumption sites), and the treatment of addiction as the medical condition it is—not a moral failing to be punished. At the same time, I support aggressive interdiction of fentanyl precursors at our borders and diplomatic pressure on the nations where those precursors originate. We must treat the demand side with compassion and the supply side with force.

I refuse to accept the death of rural healthcare.

Rural hospital closures have reached crisis levels—over 150 rural hospitals have closed or converted since 2010, and hundreds more are at risk. I support emergency federal stabilization funding for at-risk rural hospitals, the expansion of telehealth infrastructure and reimbursement, and loan forgiveness programs for healthcare professionals who practice in underserved rural areas. A zip code should not determine whether you survive a heart attack.

Labor, Unions & The Worker’s Compact

My Statement on Labor, Unions & The Worker’s Compact

I believe organized labor is the backbone of the middle class.

The decades-long assault on union power has not been a victory for the free market—it has been a transfer of leverage from workers to shareholders. The collapse of union membership since the 1970s directly correlates with the stagnation of middle-class wages, the explosion of income inequality, and the erosion of workplace safety standards. I believe that the right to organize, collectively bargain, and strike is as fundamental as any right in the Bill of Rights. Without the countervailing power of organized labor, the employment relationship is not a negotiation—it is a dictation.

I support the full passage of the PRO Act.

I advocate for the Protecting the Right to Organize Act as the essential legislative update to the NLRA for the modern economy. Specifically, I support ending the use of mandatory “captive audience” anti-union meetings, cracking down on the illegal firing of union organizers, closing the independent contractor misclassification loophole that companies use to deny workers basic protections, and strengthening the penalty structure for labor law violations so that breaking the law is no longer simply a cost of doing business.

I believe the minimum wage is a floor, not a ceiling.

I support raising the federal minimum wage and indexing it to inflation so that Congress never again has to fight the same battle twice. No one who works a full-time job in the wealthiest nation in history should live in poverty. I believe the minimum wage should be a living wage—calibrated to regional cost of living—and that the tip credit system, which allows employers to shift wage obligations to customers through tipping, must be fundamentally reformed.

I demand protections for the gig economy worker.

The rise of app-based gig work has created a new class of worker: economically dependent on a single platform but legally stripped of the protections afforded to employees. I support a federal standard that ensures gig workers who meet the functional definition of employment—consistent hours, platform control, single-source income—receive access to unemployment insurance, workers’ compensation, and the right to organize, regardless of how their contracts are classified.

I acknowledge the tension of public sector unions.

I believe in the right of public employees—including teachers, firefighters, and federal workers—to organize and bargain collectively. However, I recognize that public sector unions operate in a distinct context: they negotiate with government, and their contracts are funded by taxpayers. I believe this distinction requires transparency, not prohibition. Collective bargaining agreements in the public sector should be subject to public disclosure and democratic accountability. Where public sector unions—including police unions—use their institutional power to obstruct accountability measures or shield misconduct, I believe that specific contractual provisions must yield to the public interest. The right to organize does not include the right to insulate bad actors from consequence.

Childcare, Family Policy & The Care Economy

My Statement on Childcare, Family Policy & The Care Economy

I believe the childcare crisis is an economic crisis hiding in plain sight.

The United States asks its workers to compete in a global economy while providing virtually no public infrastructure for the care of their children. The average cost of childcare in this country rivals or exceeds the cost of in-state college tuition, and in many communities, there are simply no available slots at any price. This is not a “women’s issue”—it is a workforce participation crisis, a poverty trap, and a drag on GDP. When parents—disproportionately mothers—are forced out of the labor market because they cannot afford or find care, we lose their talent, their income, their tax contributions, and their economic potential.

I support universal pre-K and a federal childcare subsidy.

I advocate for the establishment of publicly funded universal pre-kindergarten for all three- and four-year-olds, and for a federal childcare subsidy that caps family childcare costs at no more than seven percent of household income. I believe this investment pays for itself many times over: in increased workforce participation, in reduced reliance on public assistance, in improved educational outcomes for children, and in the economic stability of families that can finally plan beyond the next paycheck.

I demand a national paid family and medical leave standard.

The United States remains the only industrialized nation on Earth with no guaranteed paid family leave. I support a federal paid family and medical leave program—covering a minimum of twelve weeks for the birth or adoption of a child, the serious illness of a family member, or the worker’s own medical needs—funded through a modest payroll contribution. No American should have to choose between caring for a newborn or a dying parent and keeping their job. This is not a radical proposition; it is the baseline standard in every other wealthy democracy on the planet, and our refusal to meet it is a policy choice, not an economic necessity.

I believe in investing in the care workforce.

The workers who care for our children, our elderly, and our disabled are among the most essential and least compensated in our economy. I support federal investment in care worker wages, training, and benefits—including home health aides, childcare workers, and early childhood educators. You cannot build a care infrastructure on poverty wages. If we value the work, we must value the workers.

Higher Education & The Debt Crisis

My Statement on Higher Education & The Debt Crisis

I believe student loan debt is a national economic crisis.

I view the $1.7 trillion student debt burden not as a personal failure of borrowers, but as a systemic failure of policy. In an economy where a degree is the new “entry minimum” for the middle class, we have allowed the cost of that ticket to skyrocket beyond affordability. I reject a system that forces an entire generation to choose between their education and their financial future. This debt prevents Millennials and Gen Z from buying homes, starting businesses, and engaging in the economy as their parents did.

I advocate for the full abolition of student loan debt.

I support a complete “Jubilee” for student borrowers. I believe that forgiving this debt is the single most effective economic stimulus we can provide to the working and middle class. By wiping the slate clean, we do not just help individuals; we energize the entire economy. We unleash billions of dollars in purchasing power that will be spent on homes, services, and new ventures, driving the growth that benefits everyone.

I anticipate—and take seriously—the objection that a full cancellation provides a windfall to high-earning professional degree holders and creates a moral hazard by removing the financial pressure that might otherwise incentivize tuition reform. These are legitimate concerns that I do not dismiss. My answer is threefold. First, the distributional data shows that the largest economic relief flows to those who attended school but did not finish, and to borrowers in the bottom half of the income distribution—not to physicians or lawyers with six-figure salaries and the earnings to service their debt. Second, cancellation alone is not the policy; it is paired with mandatory investigations into the Tuition-Industrial Complex and structural reforms to end the capitalization of interest and rein in administrative bloat. Third, a Jubilee without reform would be a moral failure; reform without a Jubilee would be justice delayed for a generation already priced out of the economy. We must do both simultaneously.

I demand an investigation into the “Tuition-Industrial Complex.”

I believe we must fix the root cause so this crisis does not repeat. I call for immediate Congressional investigations into the predatory lending practices of servicers and the administrative bloat that drives tuition inflation. I support ending the capitalization of interest that keeps borrowers in a cycle of debt. We must mitigate the rising cost of education to ensure that the next generation is not saddled with the same burden we are lifting today.

Consumer Protection & Financial Justice

My Statement on Consumer Protection & Financial Justice

I believe in ending the era of predatory banking.

I advocate for a federal “Usury Cap” on credit card interest rates. It is immoral that banks can borrow money from the Federal Reserve at nearly 4-5% and lend it to struggling families at 25% or 30%. I support capping consumer interest rates to prevent debt traps that keep the working class in perpetual servitude to financial institutions.

I declare war on “Junk Fees” and corporate gouging.

I condemn the practice of predatory overdraft fees, where banks charge $35 for a $5 coffee purchase. I support a strict federal cap on overdraft and late fees, ensuring they are proportional to the cost of the service, not a profit center for the bank. I believe Americans should keep their hard-earned money, not lose it to hidden algorithms designed to penalize the poor.

I demand a fully funded Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB).

I view the CFPB as the essential “cop on the beat” for the American consumer. I reject any attempt to defund or neuter its oversight powers. I believe in aggressive enforcement against predatory lenders, payday loan sharks, and corporate scammers. A free market cannot function without an impartial referee to blow the whistle on fraud.

Taxation & Economic Fairness

My Statement on Taxation & Economic Fairness

I believe the Tax Code should reward work, not just wealth.

I advocate for a new tax bracket for the ultra-wealthy (the top 1%). I believe that those who have benefited the most from the American system have a patriotic duty to contribute to its maintenance. I reject the “trickle-down” mythology; I believe in a tax code where a teacher or firefighter does not pay a higher effective tax rate than a hedge fund manager.

I support closing the “Unrealized Gains” loophole.

I believe it is time to tax the true income of the ultra-rich: their assets. Currently, billionaires can borrow against their massive stock portfolios tax-free, living lives of luxury without ever “realizing” a gain for the IRS. I advocate for a “Billionaire Minimum Income Tax” that captures this accumulation of wealth, ensuring that the proliferation of assets is taxed just like a paycheck.

I advocate for a competitive but fair Corporate Tax Rate.

I support raising the corporate tax rate to a level that ensures corporations pay for the infrastructure and educated workforce they rely on. However, I believe in a tiered approach: massive multinational conglomerates should not pay the same rate as a regional manufacturer. We must close offshore tax havens and ensure that companies cannot profit in America while parking their cash in the Caymans.

I pledge tax relief for the Middle Class and Main Street.

I believe in targeted tax cuts for the people who actually drive our economy.

  • For Families: I support expanding the Child Tax Credit and Earned Income Tax Credit to put cash directly back into the pockets of working parents.
  • For Small Business: I advocate for “Main Street” tax relief—allowing small family businesses to expense more investments and lowering their tax burden to help them compete against Amazon and Walmart. I believe the tax code should favor the local shop owner over the global monopoly.

Social Security, Medicare & The Promise to Our Elders

My Statement on Social Security, Medicare & The Promise to Our Elders

I believe Social Security and Medicare are sacred compacts, not optional line items.

These programs are not entitlements in the pejorative sense that word has been weaponized to mean—they are earned benefits that American workers have paid into over a lifetime of labor. I reject any proposal to cut, privatize, or “restructure” Social Security benefits, raise the retirement age, or convert Medicare into a voucher program. These are promises made to the American people, and a government that breaks its promises to its most vulnerable citizens is not a government worthy of trust.

I support strengthening Social Security by lifting the payroll tax cap.

The current system asks a teacher making $60,000 a year to pay Social Security taxes on every dollar they earn, while a hedge fund manager making $5,000,000 stops contributing after their first few weeks of January. I support eliminating the payroll tax cap on income above $400,000, ensuring that the highest earners contribute proportionally to a system they have benefited from their entire careers. This single reform can extend the solvency of the Social Security trust fund for decades without cutting a single dollar of benefits.

I believe Medicare must be empowered to negotiate drug prices aggressively.

The Inflation Reduction Act took the first historic steps toward empowering Medicare to negotiate the cost of a limited set of prescription drugs. This was a beginning, not an end. I support dramatically expanding that negotiating authority to cover all Medicare-covered medications, using the federal government’s purchasing leverage to crush the unconscionable price inflation that forces American seniors to choose between their medication and their groceries.

I advocate for expanding Medicare to cover dental, vision, and hearing.

A health insurance program that excludes teeth, eyes, and ears is not a complete health program. The absence of these essential services has cascading health consequences—untreated dental disease contributes to cardiac and diabetic complications; vision loss leads to falls and cognitive decline. Expanding Medicare to cover these services is not a luxury; it is a preventive investment that will reduce the far greater downstream costs of emergency care.

Trade & Tariffs

My Statement on Trade & Tariffs

I believe that blanket tariffs are a tax on the American family.

I reject the populist myth that tariffs are paid by foreign countries. They are paid by American businesses and American consumers. I believe that broad, indiscriminate tariffs are fundamentally inflationary; they raise the cost of everything from clothes to cars, acting as a regressive tax that hits working-class families the hardest. I refuse to fight inflation at home while stoking it with reckless trade wars abroad.

I advocate for “Precision Protectionism,” not isolationism.

I believe tariffs should be a scalpel, not a sledgehammer. I support their use only when necessary to protect vital national security interests—such as ensuring our supply of semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, or defense materials—or to combat specific instances of dumping and slave labor. I oppose using tariffs to prop up uncompetitive industries that have no strategic value, which only breeds inefficiency and stagnation.

I believe in the power of Comparative Advantage.

I recognize the economic reality that “a country that makes everything makes nothing well.” I believe the United States should focus its massive resources on what we do best—high-tech manufacturing, innovation, energy, and services—while trading with allies for what they produce most efficiently. By deepening trade ties with free nations, we lower prices for our citizens and strengthen the alliances that keep us safe.

The “Truth in Hiring” Federal Framework

My Statement on the “Truth in Hiring” Federal Framework

I demand an end to the corporate fraud of “Ghost Jobs.”

The modern job hunt has been transformed into a dystopian, algorithm-driven nightmare, and at the center of this rot is the proliferation of “ghost jobs.” These are phantom vacancies that do not actually exist but are posted perpetually by corporations to project a false narrative of rapid growth to their shareholders, or to placate an overworked internal staff with the illusion that “help is on the way.” This is not just a frustrating HR practice; it is deceptive market manipulation. It shows a fundamental contempt for the American workforce, wasting millions of cumulative hours of candidate time, and it actively distorts our national macroeconomic data. I propose the federal “Truth in Hiring” Framework, making the posting of phantom jobs a punishable offense. Corporations that weaponize fake listings to manipulate their public image or investor relations are committing securities fraud. They will face severe, compounding financial penalties enforced jointly by the Department of Labor and the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC).

I demand absolute, unmanipulated wage transparency.

A fair, efficient, and truly free labor market relies on symmetric information. For too long, corporate HR departments have systemically hoarded compensation data to maintain all negotiating leverage, intentionally lowballing applicants and stripping them of their power. This culture of wage secrecy is also one of the primary drivers of the gender and racial wage gaps. The “Truth in Hiring” Framework will federally mandate that every single job advertisement explicitly list the actual, true salary or hourly pay rate, alongside a clear summary of benefits. We will establish equal footing from the very first interaction, ensuring no American worker wastes weeks navigating a labyrinthine interview process for a role that cannot sustain their livelihood or meet their market value.

I will outlaw bad-faith compliance and phantom salary ranges.

We will not tolerate corporate evasion or bad-faith legal loopholes. The tactic of posting absurd, artificially inflated salary ranges—such as $0 to $1 million, or $50,000 to $250,000—to technically comply with state transparency laws while actively subverting their intent will be strictly illegal at the federal level. Job postings must display the actual intended pay rate or a legally bound, exceptionally narrow variance band based on concrete, stated experience metrics. Furthermore, utilizing “average” or “median” historical salaries to obscure the actual starting pay will be prohibited, as it is mathematically and inherently misleading. If a corporation cannot honestly and precisely state what they are willing to pay for a role, they forfeit the right to publicly recruit for it.

I view predatory “application tests” as uncompensated labor.

In the modern economy, the application process has been weaponized by employers to extract free, highly skilled labor from desperate candidates. Expecting applicants to complete hours of complex, functional work—whether it is writing source code, building comprehensive marketing decks, or conducting strategic financial assessments—under the guise of an “interview test” is not an evaluation; it is wage theft. The “Truth in Hiring” Framework will strictly regulate this practice. It will mandate that any functional application assessment requiring proprietary work, or exceeding a strict one-hour time limit, must be legally classified as contract work and financially compensated at the standard market rate for that role. If a corporation wants to utilize the intellectual property, time, and talent of an American worker, they will be forced to pay for it by law.

I unequivocally call for a federal ban on AI-conducted interviews.

As I have stated in my policies on Artificial Intelligence, technology must be an engine for human potential, not a dystopian replacement for human judgment and empathy. I fundamentally reject the deeply dehumanizing use of AI to conduct one-way video interviews, analyze candidate micro-expressions, or serve as the sole gatekeeper for employment. These systems are black boxes of algorithmic bias, frequently penalizing neurodivergent candidates, minorities, and older workers while hiding behind the shield of “machine objectivity.” A person’s livelihood, career trajectory, and ability to provide for their family must never be subjected to the opaque, unfeeling filtering of a data model. Evaluating human talent requires human context, intuition, and respect. The use of automated AI systems to independently screen, interview, or reject candidates will be federally banned, and companies utilizing pseudo-scientific algorithmic personality assessments will face civil rights audits.
