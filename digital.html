The Digital Frontier & Financial Security

I believe technology must be an engine for human potential, not a dystopian replacement for human judgment and empathy.

Digital Rights & Privacy

A Digital Bill of Rights, individual data sovereignty, opt-in consent, and reforming Section 230.

AI & The Workforce

An Automation Displacement Tax, a GI Bill for the AI Age, and Human-in-the-Loop safeguards.

Data Rights & Open Innovation

Consent and Compensate framework for AI training data, and defending open-source development.

Digital Security

A Right to Reality against deepfakes, mandatory AI watermarking, and Compute Governance as national security.

Financial Security & Fraud

A Civilian Anti-Fraud Task Force, Digital RICO enforcement, and fast-track restitution for victims.

Cryptocurrency

Strict KYC/AML compliance, transparent ledgers for investigators, and ending total financial anonymity.

Digital Rights & Privacy

I believe in a “Digital Bill of Rights.”

I reject the current surveillance capitalism model where citizens are mined for data without their knowledge. I advocate for a new legal framework where Individual Data Sovereignty is the law of the land.

I firmly believe that you own your digital life.

I support legislation that establishes the inherent ownership of data generated by consumers. Corporations should only be permitted to collect or utilize this data with explicit, “opt-in” consent: consent that can be revoked by the consumer at any time. In my opinion, privacy is not a luxury for the tech-savvy; it is a fundamental civil right in the digital age.

I believe in reforming—not repealing—Section 230.

Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act is the legal foundation of the modern internet, and I reject calls for its wholesale repeal. Without its core protection—that platforms are not automatically liable for every piece of user-generated content—the internet as we know it would collapse under litigation, and the primary casualties would be small platforms and independent voices, not the tech giants who can afford armies of lawyers.

However, I believe Section 230 was written for an internet that no longer exists. It was designed for neutral bulletin boards, not for algorithmically curated feeds that actively amplify content for profit. I support a targeted reform: platforms that use recommendation algorithms to actively promote or amplify content should bear a proportional share of liability for the harms caused by that amplified content. The distinction is between hosting speech—which must remain protected—and boosting speech for profit, which is an editorial and commercial act that should carry responsibility. A platform that passively hosts a post is a bulletin board. A platform that pushes that post to ten million people because rage drives engagement is something else entirely.

Artificial Intelligence & The Workforce

I believe AI is a force multiplier, not a replacement.

I view Artificial Intelligence as a tool to enhance human potential, not a weapon to discard American workers. I reject the “inevitability” of mass automation where corporate profits soar while the middle class hollows out. The goal of innovation must be to make workers more productive and wealthier, not obsolete.

I support an “Automation Displacement Tax.”

I believe that companies have a social responsibility to their workforce. I advocate for a tax penalty on corporations that lay off human workers solely to replace them with AI or automation.

  • The Mechanism: If a company replaces a human with an algorithm to boost margins, they should pay a tax on that displacement—a “Robot Tax.”
  • The Reinvestment: This revenue will be ring-fenced to fund a National Human Capital Fund—essentially a “GI Bill for the AI Age”—paying for the retraining, upskilling, and portable benefits of displaced workers. We must ensure the benefits of AI are shared by the people who built this economy, not just the shareholders who own the servers.

I believe in “Human-in-the-Loop” safeguards.

Efficiency cannot come at the cost of humanity. I support regulations requiring meaningful human oversight in critical high-stakes sectors, such as criminal justice, healthcare, and lending. No American should be denied a loan, a medical procedure, or their freedom by a “black box” algorithm they cannot question.

Data Rights & Open Innovation

I advocate for “Data Sovereignty” in AI training.

I reject the current model where Big Tech vacuums up our creative work, personal data, and likenesses for free to train models that are then sold back to us. I support a “Consent and Compensate” framework: AI companies must obtain “opt-in” consent to use personal data for training, and we should explore “Data Dividend” models where citizens are compensated when their data generates profit. You own your digital self.

I support Open Source to prevent a “Knowledge Monopoly.”

I fear a future where the most powerful intelligence in history is controlled by three or four massive corporations. I believe Open Source AI is the democratizing force that prevents this dystopia. I will block regulatory attempts by large incumbents to “pull up the ladder” and ban open-source development under the guise of safety. I believe American innovation thrives when code is accessible to students in garages, not just PhDs in Silicon Valley.

Truth & Digital Security

I propose a “Right to Reality” against Deepfakes.

I view non-consensual deepfakes—whether used for financial fraud, election interference, or sexual exploitation—as a violation of basic rights.

  • The Solution: I advocate for mandatory “Watermarking” standards for all major AI generators, ensuring synthetic content is machine-identifiable.
  • The Liability: Platforms that knowingly host or algorithmically boost criminal deepfakes must be held liable. We cannot allow social media algorithms to profit from the deception of the American public.

I believe in “Compute Governance” as National Security.

While I support open software, I believe in tight controls on the hardware. Compute is the new oil. I support strict export controls on the most advanced AI chips to adversarial nations. We must maintain a “hardware moats” strategy: America and its allies must control the physical infrastructure—fabs and data centers—that powers the future, ensuring that authoritarian regimes cannot use American technology to perfect their surveillance states.

Financial Security & Fraud

I believe that protecting our elderly is a national security issue.

I view the epidemic of international financial scams—from “pig butchering” crypto schemes to grandparent imposter fraud—not just as a crime, but as an assault on our sovereignty. In 2024 alone, Americans lost billions to these foreign syndicates. I refuse to accept a status quo where criminal networks drain the life savings of the generation that built this country.

I advocate for a “Citizen-Government Partnership” against fraud.

I recognize that federal agencies often move too slowly to catch modern cyber-criminals. I applaud the heroic work of civilian investigators and ethical hackers who expose these networks, disrupt their operations, and protect victims in real-time. I will establish a “Civilian Anti-Fraud Task Force” within the DOJ, bringing independent investigators and OSINT (Open Source Intelligence) experts to the table, using their on-the-ground intelligence to guide federal prosecutions and strategy.

We cannot arrest our way out of this; we must inoculate the public. I will enlist these trusted communicators to co-develop a national, modern educational campaign—video-first and accessible—to teach every American how to spot a scam before they become a victim.

I declare war on “Fake Gurus” and Domestic Fraud.

Scammers don’t just operate from overseas call centers; they operate from luxury condos in Miami and Los Angeles. I condemn the rise of domestic financial predators—from “rug-pull” crypto schemes to predatory investment courses—that target desperate Americans looking for financial stability.

I believe in modernizing enforcement with independent expertise.

The SEC and FTC have struggled to keep up with the speed of internet finance. I advocate for bringing independent investigative journalists into advisory roles for our regulatory bodies. These investigators often uncover billion-dollar frauds months before regulators do. By integrating their expertise into our working groups, we can identify emerging threats faster. I support stiffening penalties for domestic financial fraud. If you use social media to pump a worthless token or sell a fraudulent “get rich quick” scheme, you should face the same federal scrutiny as a bank robber. The digital age is not a license to steal.

I advocate for a “Digital RICO Mandate” to crush organized fraud.

I refuse to accept the narrative that the digital frontier—whether it involves crypto rug pulls, NFT scams, or AI-generated financial fraud—is a lawless Wild West. These are not victimless crimes; they are coordinated digital cartels. I advocate for the aggressive application and modernization of RICO statutes to prosecute these schemes. If you develop the fraudulent smart contract, if you use your social media influence to pump the worthless asset, or if you knowingly launder the proceeds, you are part of a criminal enterprise. You will be held collectively liable for the devastation you cause, regardless of your specific rung on the ladder.

I believe in piercing the veil of digital anonymity.

Fraudsters currently hide behind DAOs (Decentralized Autonomous Organizations), anonymous burner accounts, and complex algorithmic trading to shield themselves from liability. I support amending federal statutes to strip away these digital shields. We must empower the Department of Justice and the SEC to hold the individual human operators behind these digital entities personally and criminally accountable.

I demand a “Fast-Track to Made Whole” for defrauded consumers.

The current legal system makes it financially impossible for the average citizen to sue a digital scammer who has stolen their life savings. I advocate for a radical overhaul of consumer recourse in the digital age. We must amend federal law to lower the burden of proof for civil recovery in cases of established digital fraud, and mandate that seized assets from these syndicates be directly and rapidly redistributed to the victims, rather than languishing in government coffers. Consumers deserve their money back, and the law must provide a direct, accessible path to get it.

Cryptocurrency Anonymity & National Security

I believe total financial anonymity is a threat to the Republic.

I reject the ideological premise that cryptocurrency should exist in a dark regulatory void. The total anonymity of unhosted crypto wallets and decentralized accounts is not a feature of liberty; it is a glaring vulnerability in our national security. We cannot allow terrorist organizations, criminal cartels, scammers, and state-sponsored hackers to move billions of dollars through the digital shadows, bypassing the Bank Secrecy Act and the watchful eye of the government.

I demand strict KYC/AML compliance for the entire crypto ecosystem.

I advocate for a zero-tolerance policy on anonymous digital assets. Any cryptocurrency company, exchange, or wallet provider based in or operating within the United States must be legally required to implement rigorous Know Your Customer (KYC) and Anti-Money Laundering (AML) protocols. There must be a verified human identity tied to every wallet and asset. If a platform refuses to hold and verify this identifying information, they will be barred from interacting with the US financial system.

I support fully transparent ledgers for fraud investigators.

We must strip away the shield of anonymity that protects bad actors. The identifying information held by these companies must be plainly visible and immediately accessible to certified fraud investigators, financial compliance professionals, and law enforcement agencies fighting against digital predation. The technology that enables crypto should be used to perfectly trace illicit funds, not hide them. If a user seeks to benefit from the stability of the American economy, they must step into the light.

I am not blind to the civil liberties tension embedded in this framework. The same surveillance architecture that traces criminal cartels can, if improperly governed, be used to monitor political dissidents, expose domestic violence survivors fleeing their abusers, or chill the financial privacy of journalists and whistleblowers. I do not dismiss this risk; I take it seriously as someone who believes digital privacy is a fundamental right. My answer is structural: access to KYC/AML records by law enforcement must require a judicial warrant or a formal administrative subpoena process, subject to the same Fourth Amendment constraints that govern the acquisition of bank records under the Right to Financial Privacy Act. The goal is not a panopticon; it is a traceable financial system with proper legal guardrails. Transparency for criminals. Privacy for citizens.
